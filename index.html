4 secrets to lower blood sugar levels

Blood sugar levels are a big concern for people with diabetes. High blood sugar, known as hyperglycemia, occurs when a person's blood sugar level exceeds 180 milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL).

And did you know that alarming blood sugar levels can have negative consequences on the health and quality of life of "pre-diabetics" who feel symptoms without even knowing it?

In this article, we review the top 4 secrets to lowering blood sugar.

Why is blood sugar control important?

Keeping your blood sugar within your target range can help you avoid health problems with high blood sugar.

Over time, unhealthy blood sugar levels can damage small and large blood vessels in many organs and systems, which can have serious consequences, including visual impairment and blindness, foot ulcers, infections and amputations, heart failure, kidney disease and dialysis, heart attacks and strokes, diseases of the peripheral vascular system, and damage to the nervous system leading to pain and weakness.

Take a look at this staggering statistic: up to 90% of people in the UK have abnormal blood sugar levels. Hungry more than usual? Or are you thirstier than usual? Do you have pain in your upper legs or do you think your ankles and feet are swollen? If so, you have a good chance of suffering the consequences of increased blood sugar levels.

By keeping your blood sugar below 100 mg/dL before meals and below 180 mg/dL after meals, you can greatly reduce the risk of adverse effects from cravings for sugar levels. in the blood.

How to lower blood sugar

Here are 4 main ways to lower high blood sugar and reduce the risk of complications:

1- Reduce your carbohydrate intake

Researchers have conducted studies showing that eating foods low in carbohydrates and high in protein lowers blood sugar levels.

The body breaks down carbohydrates into sugars that the body uses for energy. Some carbohydrates are important in the diet. However, for many people (especially those with diabetes), eating too many carbohydrates can cause excessive jumps in blood sugar.

Reducing the amount of carbohydrates a person eats reduces swings in blood sugar.

2- Eat the right carbohydrates

The two main types of carbohydrates are simple and complex, and they affect blood sugar levels differently.

Simple carbohydrates are generally made up of a type of sugar. They are found in foods like white bread, pasta, and sweets. The body breaks down these carbohydrates into sugars very quickly, causing blood sugar levels to rise rapidly.

Carbohydrate bonds are made up of three or more types of sugar linked together. Due to the chemical bonds between these types of sugars, it takes longer for the body to break them down.

As a result, sugar leaves the body more slowly, which means that blood sugar levels do not rise as quickly after a meal. Examples of carbohydrates include whole grains and sweet potatoes.

3- Choose foods with a low glycemic index

The glycemic index measures and ranks different foods based on how they cause a spike in blood sugar levels. Research shows that following a low glycemic index diet reduces rapid jumps in blood sugar levels.

Low glycemic index foods are those with a value below 55. Examples of low glycemic index foods are: sweet potatoes, quinoa, legumes, low-fat milk, green vegetables, non-starchy vegetables, nuts and seeds, meat and fish.
